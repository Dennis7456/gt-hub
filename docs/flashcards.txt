Below are mastery flashcards built from your **Customs Clearance I** notes. I organized them from basic recall to exam-style application so you can use them for memorization, oral questioning, and quick revision. They are based on the uploaded notes. 

Use them in 3 rounds:

1. Read the **front** only and answer aloud.
2. Cover the **back** and explain it in your own words.
3. Revisit the “trap” and calculation cards until you can answer instantly.

## 1) Prohibited and restricted goods

These cards cover definitions, examples, legal effect, and handling logic from the notes. 

**1. Front:** What are prohibited goods?
**Back:** Goods that are not allowed to be imported, exported, or transferred into or outside Partner States.

**2. Front:** How does EACCMA define prohibited goods?
**Back:** Goods whose importation, exportation, or carriage coastwise is prohibited under the Act or any law in force in the Partner States.

**3. Front:** What are restricted goods?
**Back:** Goods whose importation or exportation is allowed only after specific conditions are met.

**4. Front:** What is the main difference between prohibited and restricted goods?
**Back:** Prohibited goods are not allowed at all; restricted goods are allowed only with permits, certificates, or approvals.

**5. Front:** Name four examples of prohibited goods.
**Back:** Counterfeit currency, pornographic materials, matches made with white phosphorus, narcotic drugs under international control.

**6. Front:** Which kind of false money is prohibited?
**Back:** Counterfeit currency notes and coins, and money not of the established standard in weight or fineness.

**7. Front:** Why are pornographic and obscene materials prohibited?
**Back:** Mainly for social and moral control.

**8. Front:** Why are narcotic drugs prohibited?
**Back:** For health, safety, and international control reasons.

**9. Front:** Why are hazardous wastes prohibited?
**Back:** For environmental and health protection.

**10. Front:** Give examples of restricted goods.
**Back:** Postal franking machines, traps for game animals, unwrought precious metals, arms and ammunition, worked ivory, GM products, second-hand tyres.

**11. Front:** Why are arms and ammunition restricted?
**Back:** Because of security and safety concerns.

**12. Front:** Why are endangered flora and fauna products restricted?
**Back:** To comply with CITES and protect biodiversity.

**13. Front:** Why are genetically modified products restricted?
**Back:** Because they require regulatory control and safety assessment.

**14. Front:** Why are second-hand tyres restricted?
**Back:** For safety, environmental, and quality-control reasons.

**15. Front:** Which schedule deals with prohibited and restricted exports?
**Back:** The Third Schedule.

**16. Front:** What does Section 70 of EACCMA say about prohibited exports?
**Back:** Goods specified in Part A of the Third Schedule are prohibited goods, and their exportation is prohibited.

**17. Front:** Give two examples of prohibited exports from the notes.
**Back:** Used or scrap cables and conductors of aluminium, steel, and copper.

**18. Front:** What are restricted exports?
**Back:** Goods in Part B of the Third Schedule whose export is allowed only if conditions regulating their export are met.

**19. Front:** Give five examples of restricted exports.
**Back:** Waste and scrap of ferrous cast iron, timber, fresh unprocessed Nile perch and tilapia, wood charcoal, used automobile batteries and lead scrap.

**20. Front:** What vessel-size rule applies to some restricted exports?
**Back:** Warehoused goods, goods under duty drawback, and transshipped goods shall not be exported in vessels of less than 250 tons register.

**21. Front:** What document support must exist before restricted goods are cleared?
**Back:** Import permits, certificates, or authority from the relevant agency.

**22. Front:** Who controls restricted goods besides Customs?
**Back:** Specific government agencies depending on the nature of the goods.

**23. Front:** Name six reasons goods may be prohibited or restricted.
**Back:** Political, health, economic, safety and security, environmental and agricultural, and social reasons.

**24. Front:** How should prohibited imports generally be handled?
**Back:** They should not be cleared for import; they are liable to seizure and other enforcement action.

**25. Front:** How should restricted imports generally be handled?
**Back:** They may be cleared only after the importer satisfies all permit and agency requirements.

**26. Front:** How should prohibited exports be handled?
**Back:** Exportation should be stopped because the law prohibits it.

**27. Front:** How should restricted exports be handled?
**Back:** Export is allowed only if conditions and authorizations are satisfied.

**28. Front:** Exam trap: Are all dangerous goods prohibited?
**Back:** No. Some are prohibited, while others are only restricted and may move with approval.

## 2) Core import customs procedures and basic terms

These cards cover definitions the examiner can ask directly. 

**29. Front:** What are the four import categories in the notes?
**Back:** Direct imports, temporary imports, re-imports, and warehoused goods.

**30. Front:** What is an approved place of loading/unloading?
**Back:** A quay, jetty, wharf, or other place, including part of a Customs airport, appointed by the Commissioner for loading or unloading goods.

**31. Front:** What is a boarding station?
**Back:** A place appointed by the Commissioner for aircraft or vessels to bring to for boarding or disembarkation of officers.

**32. Front:** What is cargo?
**Back:** All goods imported or exported in an aircraft, vehicle, or vessel, excluding stores for use by the craft, crew and passengers, and bona fide personal baggage.

**33. Front:** What is a Customs area?
**Back:** A place appointed under section 12 for Customs operations, including deposit of goods under Customs control.

**34. Front:** What is a Customs warehouse?
**Back:** A place approved for deposit of unentered, unexamined, abandoned, detained, or seized goods for security of goods or duties.

**35. Front:** Who is a master?
**Back:** Any person having or taking charge or command of an aircraft or vessel.

**36. Front:** What does “import” mean?
**Back:** To bring or cause to be brought into the Partner States from a foreign country.

**37. Front:** What is a port under Customs law?
**Back:** A place appointed by the Council for purposes of Customs law; for aircraft, a port means a Customs airport.

**38. Front:** Who is a proper officer?
**Back:** Any officer whose right or duty it is to require or perform acts referred to in the Act.

**39. Front:** What is a sufferance wharf?
**Back:** A place other than an approved loading or unloading place where the Commissioner may allow goods to be loaded or unloaded.

**40. Front:** What is a transit shed?
**Back:** A building or premises appointed by the Commissioner in writing for deposit of goods under Customs control.

**41. Front:** What are uncustomed goods?
**Back:** Dutiable goods whose full duty has not been paid, and any goods dealt with contrary to Customs laws whether dutiable or not.

**42. Front:** What are the three main modes of cargo arrival mentioned?
**Back:** Overland, by air, and by sea.

## 3) Arrival overland, reporting duties, and special arrivals

These cards test practical procedure. 

**43. Front:** How may cargo arrive overland according to the notes?
**Back:** By motor vehicle, train, pipeline, animal, and cables.

**44. Front:** What does Section 29 require of a person in charge of a vehicle arriving overland?
**Back:** Report arrival, furnish information, declare truth of particulars, answer questions, produce documents, and make due entry.

**45. Front:** To whom must the driver report arrival?
**Back:** The officer stationed at the frontier port where the vehicle entered the Partner State.

**46. Front:** What declaration form is mentioned for overland vehicle arrival?
**Back:** Form C17.

**47. Front:** What documents may the proper officer demand from the driver?
**Back:** Consignment notes and other relevant documents.

**48. Front:** What happens if a person conveys goods by other means, not vehicle or train?
**Back:** Under Section 31, that person must still fulfill reporting, documentation, questioning, and declaration duties similar to Section 29.

**49. Front:** How is wet cargo arriving overland treated?
**Back:** Section 32 applies; the pipeline operator must report and record the nature and quantities and provide apparatus for proper records.

**50. Front:** Give examples of wet cargo.
**Back:** Petroleum and gas.

**51. Front:** What is the Customs area for cargo arriving on an international train?
**Back:** The border railway station.

**52. Front:** Who acts on behalf of the railway company at the station?
**Back:** The station master or other person in charge.

**53. Front:** What must the station master submit on arrival of the train?
**Back:** Copies of invoices, consignment notes, waybills, and other documents relating to the goods in wagons.

**54. Front:** Can the station master allow goods to leave the Customs area without permission?
**Back:** No. That is an offence.

**55. Front:** Can goods required to be entered at another station be delivered without written permission?
**Back:** No.

**56. Front:** Through where must a person in charge bring goods into a Partner State?
**Back:** Through a port appointed under Section 11, unless otherwise permitted.

## 4) Rummaging and search powers

These cards are good for oral exams because they test sequence, legality, and officer powers. 

**57. Front:** What is rummaging?
**Back:** An intense search of a vessel or aircraft by the proper officer, usually on a risk basis.

**58. Front:** Which section gives the proper officer power to search aircraft or vessels?
**Back:** Section 246 of EACCMA.

**59. Front:** What does Regulation 25 require during rummaging?
**Back:** The crew must provide all necessary assistance to officers conducting the search.

**60. Front:** Who must be informed first when a rummage squad boards a vessel?
**Back:** The Master, or in his absence the Chief Officer.

**61. Front:** What should the officer in charge request from the Master?
**Back:** A ship’s officer to accompany the squad and crew assistance if dismantling is needed.

**62. Front:** What is checked against Form C.9 during rummage?
**Back:** Crew declarations against goods held in their quarters.

**63. Front:** Which part of a vessel should be rummaged first?
**Back:** The crew’s quarters.

**64. Front:** Why are crew quarters searched first?
**Back:** To check undeclared dutiable goods while officers are still clean enough to handle personal effects.

**65. Front:** Can living quarters be rummaged in the absence of occupants?
**Back:** No. At least one occupant must be present.

**66. Front:** When may personal effects and lockers be searched?
**Back:** Only in the presence of the owner.

**67. Front:** What caution applies when searching passenger accommodation and public rooms?
**Back:** Do not disturb passengers or damage fittings and furnishings.

**68. Front:** What caution applies when searching machinery and structural fittings?
**Back:** Do not endanger safety or mechanical operation; consult a competent ship’s officer.

**69. Front:** What should officers do if undeclared goods are found during rummage?
**Back:** Note the concealment, seize the goods, report to the officer in charge, show them to the Master, and try to trace the owner.

**70. Front:** What happens to seized goods after rummage?
**Back:** They are marked for identification and taken ashore to the Customs warehouse.

**71. Front:** What must be issued or prepared after seizure?
**Back:** A report, and possibly a Notice of Seizure and offence report.

**72. Front:** Why are coastal vessels occasionally rummaged?
**Back:** As a revenue precaution against hoarding, illicit transfers, smuggling, and evasion of prohibitions and restrictions.

**73. Front:** Are rummages of all scheduled foreign flights mandatory?
**Back:** No. Scheduled services should be rummaged minimally consistent with revenue control.

**74. Front:** Which aircraft rummages are mandatory?
**Back:** Non-scheduled, charter, and private aircraft arriving from foreign.

**75. Front:** What is the purpose of rummaging an aircraft?
**Back:** To ensure all goods, baggage, stores, and cargo are lawfully on board and properly accounted for.

**76. Front:** What happens to goods on an aircraft not accounted for to the officer’s satisfaction?
**Back:** They must be detained and the facts reported.

**77. Front:** When may a person be searched?
**Back:** When an officer has reasonable grounds to believe the person has uncustomed goods.

**78. Front:** What if the person objects to being searched?
**Back:** The person must be taken before the station manager or senior officer to decide whether the search should proceed.

**79. Front:** Where should searches of persons take place?
**Back:** In a cabin, Customs office, or other private place.

**80. Front:** Who may search a female suspect?
**Back:** Only a female officer.

## 5) Arrival by air and sea: documents and clearance

These are straight exam cards on inward reporting and clearance of vessels/aircraft. 

**81. Front:** What must the master or agent produce on arrival?
**Back:** All books and documents relating to the craft, cargo, stores, baggage, crew, and passengers as required by the proper officer.

**82. Front:** What is Form C2?
**Back:** Inward report for a vessel.

**83. Front:** What is Form C4?
**Back:** Inward report for an aircraft; it is also mentioned as the clearance form for an aircraft.

**84. Front:** What information is contained in the inward report?
**Back:** Particulars such as nationality, tonnage, crew numbers, and port of landing.

**85. Front:** What is Form C3?
**Back:** Parcels list showing packages or parcels imported for which no bill of lading was issued.

**86. Front:** What is included in the crew list?
**Back:** Full name, gender, nationality, and details of the master and crew members.

**87. Front:** What is Form C5?
**Back:** Passenger list showing passengers disembarking and those remaining on board.

**88. Front:** What is Form C6?
**Back:** Declaration Advice Consumable Stores, giving details of all dutiable stores on board.

**89. Front:** What is Form C7?
**Back:** Crew Declaration of all dutiable goods in possession of each crew member.

**90. Front:** What is the last port of call list?
**Back:** A list of the vessel’s last ten port calls in chronological order.

**91. Front:** What does the arms and ammunition list show?
**Back:** Details of all arms and ammunition on board.

**92. Front:** What does the narcotics list show?
**Back:** Details of all narcotics on board.

**93. Front:** When should the cargo manifest be submitted?
**Back:** Before arrival of the vessel or aircraft, under Section 24.

**94. Front:** What does Regulation 23 say about dutiable stores?
**Back:** Stores for crew and passengers may be produced separately and approved or placed under seal by the proper officer.

**95. Front:** What are the duty-free allowable stores per crew member?
**Back:** 200 cigarettes or 200g tobacco, 750 ml potable spirits, and 3 litres of wine or beer.

**96. Front:** What happens to quantities above the allowable stores?
**Back:** They are sealed by the proper officer.

**97. Front:** Is breaking the seal without approval an offence?
**Back:** Yes.

**98. Front:** What is issued once the proper officer is satisfied all requirements are met?
**Back:** A certificate of clearance.

**99. Front:** What fee is mentioned for certificate of clearance?
**Back:** USD 3.

## 6) Manifest amendment, supplementary manifest, short landed and excess landed cargo

These are common trick areas. 

**100. Front:** What can the proper officer permit to be amended in an inward report?
**Back:** Destination, ownership, status of goods, or obvious errors/omissions.

**101. Front:** What documents support amendment of a manifest?
**Back:** A manifest corrector and an affidavit.

**102. Front:** What fee is paid for amendment of inward report?
**Back:** USD 10.

**103. Front:** What is short landed cargo?
**Back:** Cargo that was manifested but is not physically on board for unloading.

**104. Front:** What is excess landed cargo?
**Back:** Cargo not manifested but physically on board for unloading.

**105. Front:** Name three reasons that may justify short landed cargo.
**Back:** Goods were not shipped, were discharged at a previous port, were overcarried to a later port.

**106. Front:** Give four more possible explanations for short landed cargo.
**Back:** Returned after overcarriage, carried by another craft, lost at sea, stolen or destroyed before arrival within EAC ports.

**107. Front:** How is excess landed cargo regularized?
**Back:** By a supplementary manifest.

**108. Front:** What is the fee for a supplementary manifest?
**Back:** USD 10.

## 7) Coasting vessels and aircraft

These cards test special movement within Partner States. 

**109. Front:** What does “carried coastwise” mean?
**Back:** Goods conveyed by sea or air from one part of a Partner State to another part thereof.

**110. Front:** What is a coasting vessel or aircraft?
**Back:** A vessel or aircraft carrying goods coastwise within the Partner States.

**111. Front:** Which provisions govern coasting vessels and aircraft?
**Back:** Sections 96–109 of EACCMA and Regulations 123–130 of EACCMR.

**112. Front:** What document must the master present for coastwise carriage or transfer?
**Back:** A transire.

**113. Front:** What does the transire do?
**Back:** It authorizes carriage of the goods and also serves as certificate of clearance for the coastwise voyage.

**114. Front:** In what form must the account for coastwise cargo be delivered?
**Back:** In triplicate on the prescribed form.

**115. Front:** Where must coastwise goods be loaded or unloaded?
**Back:** At an approved place of loading or a sufferance wharf.

**116. Front:** What may the proper officer require after unloading coastwise goods?
**Back:** That goods be conveyed immediately to a Customs area or transit shed.

**117. Front:** What may the proper officer require for goods transferred by road?
**Back:** Immediate conveyance to a Customs house or another directed place.

## 8) Damaged cargo, shipwreck, and goods reported but not unloaded

These are short but important. 

**118. Front:** What happens if goods reported to be unloaded are in fact not unloaded?
**Back:** The master or agent must pay duty unless acceptable explanation is given.

**119. Front:** What must a master do if a vessel or aircraft is accidentally forced to land in EAC States?
**Back:** Immediately report the cargo and stores to the nearest Customs or administrative officer.

**120. Front:** What happens to cargo and stores of an abandoned vessel or aircraft?
**Back:** They are liable to seizure unless satisfactory explanation is given.

**121. Front:** In case of loss or wreck, what must the master or agent do?
**Back:** Report the aircraft or vessel and its cargo and stores with all reasonable speed to the nearest officer.

## 9) Entry of goods and timelines

These are core exam cards because dates and definitions are often tested. 

**122. Front:** When are goods deemed to be entered under Section 2(2)(a)?
**Back:** When entry is made and lodged properly and duty or deposit is paid, or security is given.

**123. Front:** When are goods deemed to be entered for home consumption?
**Back:** When declared for use in a Partner State other than temporary use and requirements of entry are fulfilled.

**124. Front:** Within how many days must goods be entered?
**Back:** Within 21 days after commencement of discharge, or on arrival for vehicles, unless further time is allowed.

**125. Front:** What happens if cargo remains unentered after 21 days?
**Back:** It becomes liable to Customs warehouse rent.

**126. Front:** What if cargo is entered but not removed from the port within 21 days?
**Back:** It is deemed to be in a Customs warehouse.

**127. Front:** Can goods be entered before arrival?
**Back:** Yes, under Section 34(3), allowing pre-arrival processing.

**128. Front:** Why is pre-arrival processing important?
**Back:** It reduces clearance time and costs.

**129. Front:** Who bears the burden of proving correctness of entry particulars?
**Back:** The owner, not Customs.

**130. Front:** For what purposes may cargo be entered?
**Back:** Home consumption, warehousing, transhipment, transit, or export processing zones.

## 10) Supporting documents and declarations

These cards help you answer “what documents are required?” on the spot. 

**131. Front:** Name five basic supporting documents for import entry.
**Back:** Bill of lading/airway bill/road manifest, packing list, commercial invoice, freight invoice, insurance debit note.

**132. Front:** Name five more supporting documents.
**Back:** Certificate of export, certificate of conformity, registration/incorporation/ID, TIN, certificate of origin.

**133. Front:** Name five additional possible supporting documents.
**Back:** Import Declaration Form, motor vehicle logbook, certificate of roadworthiness, permits/licenses, chemical analysis report.

**134. Front:** What proof of payment may support entry?
**Back:** Telegraphic transfer, letter of credit, and similar evidence.

**135. Front:** What is Form C36?
**Back:** Declaration of Value, a statement by the importer about trading terms with the foreign supplier.

**136. Front:** Who must complete Form C36?
**Back:** The owner of the goods, not the Customs agent.

**137. Front:** Who completes Certificate A on Form C36?
**Back:** An importer not related to the supplier.

**138. Front:** Who completes Certificate B on Form C36?
**Back:** An importer related to the supplier.

**139. Front:** What is a provisional entry?
**Back:** An entry lodged when there are insufficient documents or information.

**140. Front:** What security may the Commissioner require for provisional entry?
**Back:** Cash deposit, bank guarantee, or bond.

**141. Front:** Within how long must a provisional entry be perfected?
**Back:** Within three months, or further period allowed.

**142. Front:** What are the uses of a Customs declaration?
**Back:** Tax assessment, tax payment arrangement, determination of bond in force, document processing across institutions, and obtaining genuine clearance.

**143. Front:** Why must Customs declarations be standardized internationally?
**Back:** To facilitate trade and ensure uniform minimum data requirements.

**144. Front:** Which international instrument influences declaration layout?
**Back:** The Revised Kyoto Convention through the UN layout key.

**145. Front:** What did the UN layout key lead to?
**Back:** Development of the Single Administrative Document (SAD).

## 12) Goods in transit shed

These test legal responsibility. 

**149. Front:** How are goods in a transit shed legally treated?
**Back:** They are deemed to be in the importing vessel or aircraft until delivered.

**150. Front:** Who remains responsible for goods in a transit shed?
**Back:** The owner or agent.

**151. Front:** What if reported goods are not duly unloaded and deposited in a transit shed or Customs area?
**Back:** The master or agent must pay duty unless he explains satisfactorily.

**152. Front:** Who commits an offence if goods in custody are not accounted for?
**Back:** The owner of vessel/aircraft, his agent, or the transit shed owner.

**153. Front:** Who is liable for reshipment or destruction costs of condemned goods?
**Back:** The owner of vessel or aircraft, his agent, or the transit shed owner.

**155. Front:** Give examples under imports for home use.

**158. Front:** What is EX-EPZ home use?

**Back:** Temporary importation for return in unaltered state, and temporary importation for repair.

**160. Front:** Give examples of re-import regimes.

**161. Front:** Give examples of warehousing regimes.

## 16) Sources of Customs law and EAC legal framework

These are high-value theory cards. 

**177. Front:** According to Article 39 of the Customs Union Protocol, what are sources of Customs law?
**Back:** Treaty provisions, Customs Union Protocol and annexes, Council regulations/directives, court decisions, Community Acts, and relevant principles of international law.

**178. Front:** What is a common market in simple terms?
**Back:** A group of countries working together mainly to promote trade and common interests.

**179. Front:** Which countries formed the EAC in 1967?
**Back:** Kenya, Uganda, and Tanzania.

**180. Front:** Name the presidents associated with the 1967 EAC formation.
**Back:** Jomo Kenyatta, Julius Nyerere, and Milton Obote.

**181. Front:** Give four aims of the original EAC.
**Back:** Promote trade, promote unity/cooperation, create a common market, allow free movement of goods and services.

**182. Front:** Why did the EAC collapse in 1977?
**Back:** Political differences, lack of common currency, shortage of funds, misunderstandings among presidents, and differing ideologies.

**183. Front:** When was the EAC revived according to the notes?
**Back:** 4 March 1996.

**184. Front:** Which heads of state revived the EAC?
**Back:** Yoweri Museveni, Daniel arap Moi, and Benjamin Mkapa.

**185. Front:** When was the EAC Treaty signed and when did it enter into force?
**Back:** Signed on 30 November 1999 and came into force on 7 July 2000.

**186. Front:** When was the Customs Union Protocol signed?
**Back:** 2 March 2004 in Arusha, Tanzania.

**187. Front:** What does the EAC Customs Union provide for?
**Back:** Elimination of Customs duties among Partner States, removal of non-tariff barriers, and a common external tariff for imports from foreign countries.

**188. Front:** Which countries joined later and when?
**Back:** Rwanda and Burundi joined in 2008 and began applying instruments in July 2009; South Sudan joined in 2016.

**189. Front:** What is the role of Council regulations and directives?
**Back:** They are binding on Partner States and Community organs within their jurisdictions.

**190. Front:** What is EACCMA, 2004?
**Back:** The Act providing for management and administration of Customs and related matters.

**191. Front:** What is the structure of EACCMA, 2004 according to the notes?
**Back:** 21 parts, 253 sections, and 6 schedules.

**192. Front:** What is EACCMR, 2010?
**Back:** Regulations made under Section 251 to give effect to EACCMA and govern Customs business.

**193. Front:** What is covered by the Compliance and Enforcement Regulations, 2012?
**Back:** Customs administration, cross-border cooperation, post-clearance audit, AEOs, and information management.

**194. Front:** Why must freight logistics practitioners know other national laws too?
**Back:** Because company registration, licensing, standards, phytosanitary, pharmaceutical, and poisons laws also affect operations.

## 18) Customs instruments: RKC, WTO TFA, SAFE

These cards help link theory to practical modernization. 

**203. Front:** What are Customs instruments?
**Back:** Conventions and agreements related to Customs clearance of goods.

**204. Front:** Name the three instruments emphasized in the notes.
**Back:** Revised Kyoto Convention, WTO Trade Facilitation Agreement, and WCO SAFE Framework.

**205. Front:** What is the Revised Kyoto Convention?
**Back:** An international agreement providing modern Customs procedures to facilitate legitimate trade while maintaining control.

**206. Front:** Give four key principles of the RKC.
**Back:** Transparency and predictability, standardization and simplification, simplified procedures for authorized persons, maximum use of IT.

## 21) Customs warehouse rent

These are frequently tested calculations and timelines. 

**252. Front:** What is a Customs warehouse under Section 2?
**Back:** A place approved for deposit of unentered, unexamined, abandoned, detained, or seized goods for securing duties.

**253. Front:** What is warehousing in situ?
**Back:** Treating goods as warehoused in another place ordered by the proper officer.

**254. Front:** When may warehouse rent arise?
**Back:** When goods remain unentered beyond 21 days or are entered but not removed within 21 days.

**255. Front:** Can the Commissioner waive warehouse rent?
**Back:** Yes, wholly or partly, but only in exceptional or extraordinary circumstances.

**256. Front:** How may goods in Customs warehouse be disposed of?
**Back:** Public auction, destruction, private treaty, or any method the Commissioner deems fit.

**257. Front:** Are the 21 days calendar days or working days?
**Back:** Working days; weekends and public holidays are excluded.

**258. Front:** What is the Customs Warehouse Date (CWD)?
**Back:** The 22nd working day from the date of discharge.

**259. Front:** What is the warehouse rent formula?
**Back:** CBM × number of rent days × rate per CBM × exchange rate.

**260. Front:** What simplified rate is given?
**Back:** $0.3 × exchange rate × CBM × number of days.
