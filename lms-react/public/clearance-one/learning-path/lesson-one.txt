# Topic 1: Prohibited and Restricted Goods

Topic goal: By the end of this topic, the learner should be able to:
- distinguish between prohibited and restricted goods,
- identify examples of each category,
- explain the legal basis for prohibitions and restrictions,
- apply the correct procedure when encountering such goods,
- recognise the consequences of dealing in prohibited or restricted goods.

Topic total XP: 600 base XP | Mastery bonus: +50 XP | Perfect boss bonus: +100 XP

## What are Prohibited Goods?

Prohibited goods are goods whose importation, exportation, or transit is absolutely forbidden by law. No licence, permit, or authority can make them legal to import or export.

Examples of prohibited goods:
- Counterfeit currency and forged documents
- Obscene publications and materials
- Goods that infringe intellectual property rights (e.g. pirated goods)
- Certain weapons and ammunition not permitted under any circumstances
- Goods prohibited under specific legislation (e.g. narcotics in certain categories)

Key rule: If goods are prohibited, they cannot enter or leave the country under any conditions.

## What are Restricted Goods?

Restricted goods are goods whose importation, exportation, or transit is controlled. They may be allowed, but only with the correct licence, permit, certificate, or authority from the relevant government agency.

Examples of restricted goods:
- Firearms and ammunition (require a licence)
- Medicines and pharmaceuticals (require approval from health authorities)
- Plants and plant products (require phytosanitary certificates)
- Animals and animal products (require veterinary certificates)
- Certain chemicals and hazardous materials
- Endangered species and wildlife products (CITES permits)

Key rule: Restricted goods can be imported or exported, but only with the proper documentation and authority.

## Prohibited vs Restricted — Key Distinction

| Feature | Prohibited Goods | Restricted Goods |
|---|---|---|
| Can they be imported/exported? | No — never | Yes — with proper authority |
| Is a licence/permit possible? | No | Yes |
| What happens if found? | Seizure and forfeiture | Seizure unless documents produced |

## Legal Basis

The authority to prohibit or restrict goods comes from:
- The East African Community Customs Management Act (EACCMA)
- National legislation (e.g. Customs and Excise Act)
- Partner Government Agency (PGA) regulations
- International conventions and treaties

## Consequences of Dealing in Prohibited or Restricted Goods

- Seizure and forfeiture of the goods
- Criminal prosecution
- Fines and penalties
- Imprisonment in serious cases
- Loss of import/export licences

## Practical Application

When a Customs officer encounters goods at the border:

1. Check if the goods appear on the prohibited goods list — if yes, seize immediately.
2. If goods are restricted, ask for the relevant licence, permit, or certificate.
3. If the importer/exporter cannot produce the required documents, detain the goods pending investigation.
4. Record all actions taken and report to the supervising officer.

## Summary

- Prohibited goods = absolutely forbidden, no exceptions.
- Restricted goods = allowed only with the correct authority/documentation.
- Always check the relevant schedules and PGA requirements before releasing goods.
- Failure to enforce these rules has serious legal and professional consequences.
